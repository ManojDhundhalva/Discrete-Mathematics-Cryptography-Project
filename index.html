Introduction :
•
The Data Encryption Standard (DES) is a historically significant encryption algorithm that has shaped the field of cryptography.
•
Developed in the 1970s by IBM, DES became the most widely used symmetric key algorithm for securing sensitive data.
•
Its strength lies in the use of a 56-bit key and a series of complex operations to convert plain text into cipher text.

Key Generation:
•
DES uses a 56-bit key, but it actually operates on a 64-bit key, with 8 bits used for parity checking.
•
The key goes through a process called key schedule, which involves permutation and shifting operations to generate the subkeys.
•
The key is subjected to a permutation known as the "permuted choice 1" (PC-1) to remove the parity bits and generate a 56-bit key.
❷
Key Schedule:
•
The 56-bit key is divided into two halves, known as C0 and D0, each containing 28 bits.
•
A set of 16 subkeys, each consisting of 48 bits, are derived from these halves.
•
In each round, the halves are rotated left by either one or two positions based on a predefined schedule.
•
After the rotations, a permutation called "permuted choice 2" (PC-2) is applied to generate the 48-bit subkey for that round.
❸
Initial Permutation (IP) :
•
The plaintext to be encrypted is divided into 64-bit blocks.
•
The initial permutation (IP) rearranges the bits in the block according to a predefined permutation table.
•
This permutation serves to distribute the bits across the block and provide diffusion.
❹
Feistel Network:
•
DES employs a Feistel network structure, where each block is divided into two halves, known as Left (L) and Right (R).
•
The Feistel function operates on the Right half using a 48-bit subkey derived from the key schedule, producing a 32-bit output.
•
The output is then XORed with the Left half and the Right and Left halves are swapped.
❺
Feistel Function :
•
The Feistel function takes a 32-bit input and performs the following operations:
•
Expansion Permutation (E-box) : Expands the 32-bit input to 48 bits by duplicating certain bits.
•
The expansion is defined by a permutation table.
•
XOR with Subkey : The expanded input is XORed with the corresponding 48-bit subkey obtained from the key schedule.
•
Substitution Boxes (S-boxes) : The XORed result is divided into eight 6-bit blocks, each passed through a different S-box.
•
Each S-box substitutes the 6 bits with 4 bits based on a lookup table unique to that S-box.
•
The substitution is based on the row and column values derived from the input bits.
•
Permutation (P-box) : The 32-bit output of the S-boxes is rearranged according to a predefined permutation table.
•
This permutation helps to further diffuse the bits.
❻
16 Rounds :
•
DES iterates the Feistel network 16 times, using a different subkey in each round.
•
The output of the Feistel network in the 16th round is not swapped.
❼
Final Permutation (IP-1) :
•
After completing the 16 rounds, a final permutation is applied to the swapped halves of the last round's output.
•
The final permutation is the inverse of the initial permutation, ensuring reversibility.
❽
Output :
•
The final permuted block is the ciphertext, representing the encrypted form of the plaintext.
❐
Reversing the Algo.
•
The same process is applied, but the subkeys are used in reverse order.
•
The encryption subkeys are used in decryption by reversing their order.
❐
Decryption
•
DES (Data Encryption Standard) decryption algorithm takes a 64-bit ciphertext as input.
•
The ciphertext is first subjected to an initial permutation (IP) to rearrange its bits.
•
The permuted ciphertext is divided into two 32-bit halves, left and right.
•
The decryption process involves performing 16 rounds of operations, where each round involves key mixing, substitution, and permutation..
•
In each round, the right half is expanded and XORed with a 48-bit round key derived from the original encryption key.
•
The result is passed through S-boxes for substitution and then subjected to a permutation (P) before being XORed with the left half. The process is repeated for 16 rounds in reverse order.
•
Finally, the left and right halves are swapped, and the resulting 64-bit block undergoes a final permutation (IP-1) to obtain the plaintext.
❖
Weaknesses
•
The limitations of DES (Data Encryption Standard) include its small key size of 56 bits, making it vulnerable to brute-force attacks with modern computing power.
•
DES has a fixed block size of 64 bits, limiting its ability to handle larger data efficiently.
•
It lacks built-in support for secure key management and distribution.
•
considered outdated due to advancements in cryptography, and more secure algorithms like AES(Advanced Encryption Standard) are recommended for modern applications.
